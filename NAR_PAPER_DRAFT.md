## Abstract

Aquatic invertebrates constitute the productive foundation of global aquaculture, supplying over 28 million tonnes of food annually, yet viral diseases remain the largest source of economic loss in the sector. Despite the rapid accumulation of viral genomic data from metagenomic and metatranscriptomic surveys of marine and freshwater invertebrates, no centralized knowledge base provides systematically curated, literature-traceable virus–host associations for this ecologically and economically critical group. Here we present AquaVir-KB, a comprehensive literature-mined evidence index dedicated to viruses infecting aquatic invertebrates. The database integrates 1,647 virus species spanning eight aquatic invertebrate phyla (Arthropoda, Mollusca, Cnidaria, Echinodermata, Porifera, Annelida, Platyhelminthes, and Rotifera), 14,540 target viral isolates (17,867 total), 27,096 proteins, and 353,160 structured evidence records (329,090 non-rejected) text-mined from 9,065 publications (92.0% PMID-traceable, 90.4% DOI-traceable) spanning 1950–2026. An additional 336 ICTV-recognized cross-phylum virus species and 73 viruses of unknown host phylum are catalogued. Built on a seven-layer, 140-table, 45-view relational architecture, AquaVir-KB combines ICTV-aligned taxonomy, NCBI sequence integration, genome annotation, protein functional domain classification (87.9% of 27,096 proteins domain-inferred), phylogenetic analysis (1,260 RdRP sequences), and geographic distribution data (63 countries) within a single resource. Evidence records are explicitly stratified by origin: 13,121 primary-evidence records (3.7%; derived from laboratory and field studies) and 336,512 secondary-evidence records (95.3%; derived from automated literature mining across both primary research and review articles), supplemented by 3,527 database-annotation records (1.0%). The database further assigns an automated evidence strength grade reflecting information-source quality—high (records where the cited publication reports experimental infection, virus isolation, or pathology visualization; n = 46,031, 13.1% of effective records), medium (records where the cited publication mentions a specific virus–host association with molecular or pathological context; n = 303,337, 86.5%), and low (records from metagenomic co-occurrence or computational inference without host confirmation; n = 1,348, 0.4%)—and tracks curation status (26.5% manual_checked, 42.1% auto_imported, 2.3% needs_review, 29.1% rejected). The 24,070 rejected records (6.8% of total) include bibliographic headers, non-virus bacterial content (e.g., Vibrio spp.), and encoding-garbled text captured by over-aggressive regex extraction; an ongoing garbage-rejection audit is expanding this category. All evidence grades are assigned by automated keyword-based extraction and should be interpreted as triage signals rather than definitive expert assessments. AquaVir-KB provides 1,254 virus entries (76.1%) with full public access and 347 catalog-level entries (21.1%) available upon registration, with 46 internal-development entries (2.8%) reserved for ongoing curation. The database is freely accessible via a REST API with full-text search, provides bulk data downloads (CC-BY 4.0), and supports Docker-based local deployment. AquaVir-KB addresses a critical unmet need by providing the first literature-traceable, evidence-graded knowledge base for aquatic invertebrate virology, while transparently acknowledging that the majority of its evidence records derive from automated text mining rather than manual expert curation.

## Introduction

**Paragraph 1: The Economic and Ecological Importance of Aquatic Invertebrate Viruses.** Aquatic invertebrates form the productive backbone of global aquaculture—the fastest-growing food production sector worldwide—contributing 28.9 million tonnes annually, comprising 11.2 million tonnes of crustaceans valued at approximately USD 78 billion and 17.7 million tonnes of mollusks [1]. Viral diseases are the single largest source of economic loss in invertebrate aquaculture. White spot syndrome virus (WSSV; family *Nimaviridae*) has caused cumulative global losses estimated at USD 15–20 billion since its emergence in the early 1990s, devastating shrimp farming operations across Asia and the Americas [2, 3]. Taura syndrome virus (TSV; *Dicistroviridae*) emerged in Ecuador in 1992 and spread throughout the Americas within a decade, causing regional production collapses with mortality rates exceeding 90% in susceptible stocks [5]. Infectious hypodermal and hematopoietic necrosis virus (IHHNV; *Parvoviridae*) causes growth retardation and cuticular deformities in *Penaeus vannamei*, resulting in substantial economic losses through reduced harvest weight rather than direct mortality [6]. Yellow head virus (YHV; *Roniviridae*) has caused episodic mass mortality events in *Penaeus monodon* culture across Southeast Asia since 1990 [3]. In mollusks, ostreid herpesvirus 1 (OsHV-1; *Malacoherpesviridae*) and its microvariant genotype OsHV-1 μVar have driven mass mortality events in Pacific oyster (*Crassostrea gigas*) hatcheries and grow-out operations across France, the United Kingdom, Ireland, Australia, and New Zealand since 2008, with mortality rates frequently reaching 80–100% in spat and juveniles [8, 7]. Haliotid herpesvirus 1 (HaHV-1) causes acute ganglioneuritis in farmed and wild abalone (*Haliotis* spp.), with outbreaks documented in Taiwan and Australia [9].

Beyond the aquaculture context, aquatic invertebrates harbor an immense and largely uncharacterized viral diversity. Metagenomic and metatranscriptomic surveys have revealed that marine invertebrates host thousands of novel RNA and DNA viruses spanning all major viral genome types and representing numerous previously undescribed lineages [10, 11]. Coral holobionts alone are estimated to contain thousands of uncharacterized viral populations that may play critical roles in coral health and disease [15]. Sponges—among the most ancient metazoans—host diverse viromes that may contribute to their remarkable chemical defense systems [10]. Echinoderms, including commercially valuable sea cucumbers (*Apostichopus japonicus*) subject to intensive aquaculture in China, harbor novel picornaviruses and parvoviruses of unknown pathogenic potential. Despite this dual economic and biological significance, a centralized, systematically curated knowledge base for aquatic invertebrate viruses has been conspicuously absent.

**Paragraph 2: Contributions and Limitations of Existing Resources.** Several publicly available databases serve adjacent functions but do not address the specific need for curated, evidence-graded virus–host association data for aquatic invertebrates. NCBI Virus provides the broadest sequence coverage with links to GenBank records and metadata, but it does not offer structured evidence grading, curated host verification, or literature traceability beyond the associated GenBank reference. The International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses (ICTV) Virus Metadata Resource (VMR) provides authoritative taxonomic classification for all recognized virus species [27, 28], yet its host metadata are limited to coarse categorical annotations (e.g., "invertebrates," "vertebrates," "plants") that do not resolve aquatic from terrestrial invertebrate hosts or provide species-level host specificity information. Critically, the ICTV VMR contains no literature traceability at the per-record level. Virus-Host DB [16] computationally infers virus–host associations from NCBI Taxonomy fields associated with GenBank records. While achieving broad coverage (over 40,000 virus–host pairs), these computationally derived associations lack primary literature verification and offer no evidence quality stratification. This limitation is particularly acute for aquatic invertebrate viruses, where host metadata in GenBank records are frequently incomplete, ambiguous, or absent. The Reference Viral Database (RVDB) [17] clusters RNA virus sequences at nucleotide and protein levels but provides no curated host associations. IMG/VR v3 [18] serves the viral metagenomics community with environmental virus sequence catalogs (2.3 million uncultivated viral genomes) but lacks structured host metadata beyond environmental provenance. The ViPTree server [19] provides phylogenetic placement services for novel viral sequences but does not curate host information.

These resources share a fundamental limitation: none distinguishes between a virus experimentally confirmed as a pathogen through Koch's postulates, a virus detected by RT-PCR in association with clinical disease, and a virus identified solely as a sequence in a metatranscriptomic survey. For aquatic invertebrate virology—a field in which, as our data demonstrate, 99.8% of known virus species have been discovered through metagenomic or metatranscriptomic approaches rather than classical isolation and culture (only 4 of 1,647 active species have been isolated: WSSV, YHV, TSV, IHHNV)—this distinction is critical. Researchers, biosecurity agencies, and aquaculture stakeholders require an explicit, transparent measure of the strength of evidence supporting each claimed virus–host association.

**Paragraph 3: The Present Work.** Here we describe AquaVir-KB, a comprehensive, literature-traceable knowledge base designed to fill this gap. The database was constructed through systematic integration of six primary data sources: (i) the ICTV Virus Metadata Resource (MSL41, 2025 release); (ii) NCBI GenBank, RefSeq, and Nucleotide databases; (iii) Europe PMC and PubMed literature databases; (iv) InterPro, Pfam, KEGG, and UniProt protein annotation resources; (v) the NCBI Sequence Read Archive (SRA) for metagenomic data; and (vi) GBIF and OBIS for biogeographic host occurrence data. These data were organized into a seven-layer relational architecture designed to separate concerns while maintaining referential integrity through foreign key constraints. A central methodological contribution is the explicit multi-dimensional evidence stratification (origin: primary/secondary/database; strength: high/medium/low; curation status: manual_checked/auto_imported/needs_review/rejected) applied to every virus–host association, coupled with a finer-grained `host_association_method` classification (confirmed_infection, pathology_observation, disease_outbreak, co_occurrence_metagenomic, environmental_sample) in infection records. All data modifications are tracked in curation audit logs, and all records carry provenance annotations linking to their source databases. Below, we describe the database architecture, data integration methodology, quality control procedures, and the content of the first public release.

## Materials and Methods

### 1. Data Collection, Verification, and Management

**ICTV Taxonomy Integration.** The complete ICTV Master Species List (MSL41, released March 2025) was downloaded from the ICTV website and imported into the `ictv_taxonomy` and `ictv_vmr` tables. The ICTV database, maintained as the authoritative reference for virus taxonomy [27, 28], provides the taxonomic framework to which all AquaVir-KB virus entries are mapped. This yielded 17,554 taxonomy records spanning all ICTV-recognized taxonomic ranks (realm through species) and 19,271 virus metadata records. A systematic cross-referencing procedure matched ICTV species names against the existing `virus_master` table using exact and normalized match strategies, establishing 445 ICTV-mapped status records and 524 VMR mappings. A gap analysis identified 40 ICTV-listed aquatic invertebrate-associated viruses absent from the initial database build—predominantly 22 Aquambidensovirus asteroid1–22 (Parvoviridae) from Echinodermata, 11 crustacean and mollusk parvoviruses and iridoviruses, 7 mollusk-associated ourmiaviruses and malacoherpesviruses, and 1 rotifer-associated birnavirus. These were manually curated and imported with full ICTV taxonomic lineage, GenBank accession links, host phylum assignments, and data provenance records.

**NCBI GenBank and RefSeq Integration.** Viral nucleotide accessions were retrieved from NCBI using Entrez E-utilities. Query strategies employed both targeted organism-level searches (e.g., "*Crassostrea gigas*"[Organism] AND viruses[filter]) and broad keyword-based searches across host common names, scientific names, and taxonomic groups (oyster, mussel, abalone, clam, scallop, coral, jellyfish, sea cucumber, starfish, sea urchin, sponge, polychaete, rotifer). Searches were executed against the NCBI Nucleotide database with date filters to capture both historical and recent (2023–2026) accessions. Results were batch-validated using NCBI ESummary (rettype=docsum, retmode=xml), extracting AccessionVersion, Organism, Title, Length, and associated BioProject/BioSample identifiers. Redundant accessions (identical accession numbers with different version suffixes) were deduplicated at the accession level. The final dataset comprises 17,867 viral isolates (14,540 target isolates via the `analysis_target_isolates` view, 8,771 strict-target after excluding curation conflicts), of which 3,582 have associated nucleotide sequence data in the target set, 8,590 possess complete or verified sequence completeness status, and 9,193 isolate–reference links are maintained in the `isolate_reference_links` table.

**Literature Collection and Automated Evidence Extraction.** Systematic literature searches were executed against Europe PMC and PubMed using 16 query strategies covering eight target phyla (Supplementary Table S2). Representative queries included: "(coral OR jellyfish OR sea anemone OR hydra OR cnidarian) AND (virome OR virus OR viral) AND (PUB_YEAR:[2018 TO 2026])" for Cnidaria; "("sea cucumber" OR starfish OR "sea urchin" OR echinoderm) AND (virus OR virome OR viral)" for Echinodermata; and "(oyster AND (virome OR "novel virus" OR herpesvirus)) AND (PUB_YEAR:[2020 TO 2026])" for Mollusca. Retrieved publications were deduplicated by PMID (primary key) and DOI (secondary key). After deduplication, 9,065 unique references were retained (92.0% PMID coverage, 90.4% DOI coverage, spanning 1950–2026). Of these, 524 references (5.8%) lack both PMID and DOI; these are predominantly grey literature (institutional reports, GenBank direct submissions, WOAH/CABI/NACA technical reports) and are flagged in the database as requiring external verification.

**Important methodological note on evidence provenance.** All evidence records carry an `observation_type` field that records how the evidence was obtained: `review` (n = 319,946, 90.6%) indicates evidence extracted by automated review of published literature — including both primary research articles and secondary review papers — using the `smart_match_re_extract` pipeline; `lab` (n = 13,109, 3.7%) denotes evidence directly extracted from experimental methods sections; `database_annotation` (n = 3,527, 1.0%) covers computationally inferred annotations from external databases; `field` (n = 12, <0.1%) represents field surveillance data; and NULL/`unknown` accounts for the remainder. We emphasize that `observation_type='review'` is an extraction-method label, not a judgment about whether the source publication is a review article or primary research. To address potential misinterpretation, we have added an `evidence_origin` classification that distinguishes primary evidence (laboratory and field observations, n = 13,121, 3.7%) from secondary evidence (literature-mined statements from all publication types, n = 336,512, 95.3%) and database-derived annotations (n = 3,527, 1.0%). This transparent stratification allows users to filter evidence by provenance according to their use case.

Structured evidence extraction employed a multi-stage, progressively deepening pipeline. Stage 1 applied automated keyword and regular expression pattern matching against publication titles and abstracts available via Europe PMC, extracting virus name mentions, host name mentions, detection method keywords, and numerical values (prevalence rates, mortality rates, temperature ranges). Stage 2 processed full-text XML for 4,371 downloadable articles, parsing section-structured content for detailed evidence extraction. Stage 3 implemented a fuzzy token-matching algorithm against controlled virus name and host name vocabularies. Evidence type classification employed 60+ regular expression patterns across 10 categories (host_range, diagnosis, pathogenicity, mortality, temperature, natural_infection, transmission, outbreak, virulence, other). Evidence strength assignment followed a hierarchical keyword-based decision tree applied during automated text mining: records matching experimental infection keywords (e.g., "Koch's postulate", "experimental infection", "virus isolation", "TEM visualization") were assigned 'high'; records matching molecular detection or genomic characterization keywords (e.g., "PCR detection", "whole genome sequencing", "histopathology") were assigned 'medium'; and records lacking these indicators were assigned 'low'. **Users should interpret these automated grades as information-source signals for triage and hypothesis generation, not as validated assessments of experimental evidence quality.** The evidence strength of any individual record should be verified against the linked source publication. After iterative quality hardening—including duplicate suppression with quarantine tracking (22,469 records in `evidence_dedup_quarantine`), truncated-fragment rejection (30 records), and curation status upgrades—the complete pipeline produced 353,160 structured evidence records, of which 250,269 are non-rejected (effective), with 26.5% manual_checked, 42.1% auto_imported, 2.3% needs_review, and 29.1% rejected. We note that because the same experimental finding may be cited by multiple publications, duplicate counting of the same underlying observation across different citing papers is possible and is partially addressed by our deduplication quarantine; complete resolution would require claim-level entity resolution across the full corpus, which remains ongoing work.

**Quality Control and Data Hygiene.** Duplicate virus entries (n = 14 groups) arising from numeric ICTV VMR placeholder records and case-variant naming during multi-source import were identified and resolved through canonical name matching and evidence reassignment to canonical entries, with source entries archived as `duplicate_ictv_vmr_placeholder` or `duplicate_alias_placeholder` with `public_visibility='internal'`. Non-target records (n = 1,808; viruses of algae, vertebrates, fungi, terrestrial plants, and non-aquatic organisms) inadvertently incorporated during broad taxonomic import sweeps were identified and flagged with `is_crustacean_virus=0` and `entry_type='non_target'`, excluding them from all public-facing views and data exports. A stratified random sampling validation of auto-imported evidence records assessed format and completeness criteria, with overall precision >89%.

### 2. Sequence Data Acquisition and Genome Processing

Viral nucleotide sequences were retrieved from NCBI Nucleotide and RefSeq for all 3,582 target isolates with sequence data. Genome metadata—including genome length, GC content (%), molecule type, sequence completeness, and associated BioProject/BioSample identifiers—were extracted from GenBank flat file annotations via EFetch. Genome length quality was assessed against expected ranges for each viral family based on ICTV reference data. Protein-coding sequences (CDS) were extracted from GenBank feature tables, yielding 27,096 viral protein accessions with validated amino acid translations (mean length: 373 aa, range: 19–7,195 aa).

For isolates lacking curated CDS annotations in GenBank—predominantly recently deposited metagenomic assemblies—open reading frame (ORF) prediction was performed using Prodigal v2.6.3 [25] in metagenomic mode, generating 61,339 reannotated ORFs with genomic coordinates and translated amino acid sequences. Reannotated ORFs were cross-validated against existing CDS annotations where available to assess Prodigal prediction accuracy.

### 3. Genome Annotation, Non-redundant Protein Database, and Core Gene Identification

**Non-redundant Protein Clustering.** To reduce sequence redundancy across the 27,096 viral proteins while preserving functional diversity, proteins were clustered using CD-HIT v4.8.1 [26] at a 95% amino acid identity threshold. This produced 16,730 non-redundant (NR) protein clusters, with a cluster size distribution ranging from singletons (n = 12,847 clusters) to large multi-member families. Each cluster was assigned a representative sequence stored in the `nr_protein_clusters` table.

**Core Gene Identification.** Core viral genes were identified through profile Hidden Markov Model (HMM) searches against the Pfam v36.0 database and NCBI Conserved Domain Database (CDD). The Pfam HMM library was searched using hmmscan (HMMER v3.3.2) [24] with an E-value reporting threshold of 1 × 10⁻⁵ and a per-sequence reporting threshold of 1 × 10⁻³. Core gene classification was based on the presence of Pfam domains associated with essential viral functions: RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (PF00680, RdRP_1; PF00978, RdRP_2; PF02123, RdRP_3; PF05183, RdRP_4), viral capsid (PF08762, VP4_helical; PF03865, coat; PF04426, SDom), viral helicase (PF01443, Viral_helicase1; PF04860, ResIII), and viral protease (PF08715, Peptidase_C3; PF05415, Peptidase_C30). A total of 3,642 proteins met these criteria and were classified as core viral genes in the `core_genes` table.

**Genome Annotation Standards.** Genome type classification followed ICTV genome composition standards with seven categories: ssRNA(+) (n = 615), dsDNA (n = 155), dsRNA (n = 48), ssDNA (n = 33), ssDNA(+/−) (n = 22), ssRNA(−) (n = 17), and other/unknown (n = 44). Viral family-level classification was assigned based on ICTV taxonomy for recognized species and on the consensus of NCBI Taxonomy and phylogenetic evidence for unclassified species. Genus-level classification was available for 53 distinct genera across 63 unique families.

### 4. Protein Functional Annotation and Domain Architecture Analysis

**Conserved Domain Annotation.** All 27,096 viral proteins were submitted to the NCBI Conserved Domain Database (CDD) batch search service via Entrez E-utilities, using the efetch utility with rettype=ftable. Domain hits were parsed to extract domain accession, domain name, description, start and end positions, and E-value. The batch search yielded 71,537 domain assignments across all proteins, with the majority derived from NCBI CDD (including COG, KOG, SMART, Pfam, TIGRFAM), supplemented by protein name inference, UniProt keyword-to-domain mappings, and rule-based classification.

**Functional Category Assignment Pipeline.** A rules-based inference engine was developed to map domain names and descriptions to seven functional categories (RdRP, replication, structural, host_interaction, metabolism, assembly, and unknown) using a hierarchical set of 50+ regular expression patterns. After the full pipeline execution, 23,806 proteins (87.9%) received functional category assignments via the `functional_annotation_status` classification (23,798 via `functional_category`): 5,809 RdRP (21.4%), 8,783 structural (32.4%), 5,502 replication (20.3%), 1,863 metabolism (6.9%), 1,780 host_interaction (6.6%), and 61 assembly (0.2%). The remaining 3,290 proteins (12.1%) are annotated as `unannotated`, predominantly representing uncharacterized ORFs and hypothetical proteins from metagenomic assemblies. We note that all functional annotations are computational domain-inferred predictions, not experimental validations.

**InterPro, KEGG, and Gene Ontology Integration.** Protein sequences were queried against InterPro v97.0 [47] via the InterPro REST API, yielding domain annotations for 427 proteins with associated Gene Ontology (GO) term mappings (3,452 annotations across 176 unique GO identifiers). KEGG Orthology (KO) assignments were retrieved for 2,814 proteins via the KEGG REST API [48], with 4,294 protein-pathway links across 924 unique KEGG pathways. UniProt annotations were retrieved for 11,351 proteins via UniProt REST API [46], providing standardized protein names, gene symbols, EC numbers, and GO term cross-references. The most abundant GO terms reflected viral structural and replication biology: viral envelope (GO:0019031, n = 499), viral capsid (GO:0019028, n = 248), ATP binding (GO:0005524, n = 199), RNA-directed RNA polymerase activity (GO:0003968, n = 91), and viral RNA genome replication (GO:0039694, n = 45). Three-dimensional structural models were predicted for 52 representative proteins using ESMFold [58] via the ESMFold API, with pLDDT scores recorded for each prediction.

### 5. Phylogenetic and Evolutionary Relationship Analysis

**RdRp Sequence Collection and Alignment.** RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp) sequences constitute the most conserved and phylogenetically informative gene across RNA viruses and represent the largest functional category in AquaVir-KB (5,809 proteins). For 131 representative RdRp sequences from unclassified or ambiguously classified viruses—selected to maximize taxonomic and phylum diversity—phylogenetic analysis was performed. Reference RdRp sequences from ICTV-recognized taxa were retrieved from the NCBI RefSeq database and the PalmDB curated RdRp collection. Multiple sequence alignment was performed using MAFFT v7.520 [20] with the L-INS-i algorithm (`--maxiterate 1000 --localpair`), selected for its accuracy on highly divergent sequences with variable domain architecture. Alignment quality was assessed using trimAl v1.4 [50] with the `-automated1` heuristic to remove poorly aligned columns.

**Maximum-Likelihood Phylogenetic Inference.** Maximum-likelihood trees were constructed using IQ-TREE v2.2.0 [21] with automatic model selection via ModelFinder [22], which assesses 286 DNA models or 546 protein models and selects the best-fit model according to Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). Branch support was evaluated using 1,000 ultrafast bootstrap replicates [21] and the Shimodaira-Hasegawa approximate likelihood ratio test (SH-aLRT) [23]. Family-level classification was assigned based on monophyletic clustering (bootstrap support ≥70% and SH-aLRT ≥80%) with known reference taxa. The classification pipeline yielded family-level assignments across 18 viral families (Sedoreoviridae, Roniviridae, Dicistroviridae, Picornaviridae, Marnaviridae, Solemoviridae, Flaviviridae, Totiviridae, and others) with support values recorded in the `rdrp_classification` and `rdrp_classification_v2` tables.

**Taxonomic Orphan Management.** Sixty-eight viruses (4.1% of 1,647 active species) currently lack family-level classification. These taxonomic orphans are predominantly metagenomically discovered sequences that fall outside established viral clades in reference phylogenies.

### 6. Applied Knowledge Base Construction

**Seven-Layer Relational Architecture.** AquaVir-KB is built on a seven-layer, 140-table, 45-view relational data model (Figure 1) designed to separate data concerns while maintaining referential integrity through foreign key constraints. Layer 1 (Core Virus) contains virus taxonomy, genome type, discovery context, and ICTV mappings in `virus_master` (1,647 active species; 1,254 public, 347 limited, 46 internal), `viral_isolates` (17,867 total; 14,540 target), `viral_proteins` (27,096 proteins), and `virus_ictv_mappings`. Layer 2 (Host) catalogs aquatic invertebrate host species with associated taxonomy profiles, biology profiles, and ecological trait records. Layer 3 (Evidence) contains structured literature-mined evidence in `evidence_records` (353,160 records) stratified by origin (primary/secondary/database) and evidence strength (high/medium/low), `infection_records`, `host_range_evidence`, `pathogenicity_evidence`, `outbreak_events`, `diagnostic_methods`, and `environmental_evidence`. Layer 4 (Literature) stores bibliographic metadata in `ref_literatures` (9,065 references, 92.0% PMID coverage) with associated full-text source tracking (9,867 fulltext sources, 22,261 fulltext sections). Layer 5 (Protein) integrates functional annotations (71,537 domain assignments), InterPro, KEGG, UniProt, and protein 3D structure predictions. Layer 6 (Geography/Ecology) records sample collection metadata (63 countries, 4,326/10,302 profiles with coordinates), geography quality profiles, GBIF and OBIS occurrence records. Layer 7 (Curation) implements provenance tracking (`data_provenance`, 100,599 records), curation audit logging (`curation_logs`, 1,311 entries), and data quality management.

**Evidence Grading Architecture.** The database's core methodological contribution is the explicit, multi-dimensional evidence stratification embedded in the `evidence_records` table. Each of the 353,160 structured evidence records carries three orthogonal classifications: (i) an `evidence_origin` field distinguishing primary evidence from laboratory and field observations (n = 13,121, 3.7%) versus secondary evidence from automated literature mining (n = 336,512, 95.3%) versus database-derived annotations (n = 3,527, 1.0%); (ii) an `evidence_strength` field (high/medium/low) assessing the reliability of the source from which the record was derived; and (iii) a `curation_status` field (auto_imported/manual_checked/needs_review/rejected) tracking human verification.

Because 95.3% of evidence records originate from automated text mining of published literature rather than direct experimental data entry, the `evidence_strength` grading reflects the **information-source quality** of the underlying record rather than an independent assessment of experimental rigor. High-grade evidence (n = 46,031, 13.1% of effective records) identifies records where the cited publication reports experimental infection, virus isolation, transmission electron microscopy visualization, or multi-factor pathogen confirmation—as determined by keyword-based automated extraction and, for a subset, subsequent manual verification. Medium-grade evidence (n = 303,337, 86.5%) identifies records where the cited publication mentions a specific virus–host association with a detectable molecular or pathological context (e.g., PCR-based detection, histopathology, genomic characterization, disease outbreak report); this tier is assigned automatically during text mining and covers the majority of published observations. Low-grade evidence (n = 1,348, 0.4%) identifies records derived from metagenomic co-occurrence, computational inference, or environmental sampling without explicit host association verification. An additional 102,891 records (29.1%) are marked as `rejected`—comprising non-target virus evidence (n = 33,587), bibliographic header/garbage text extraction artifacts (n = 21,128), non-viral content including bacterial, fungal, plasmid, and barcode marker sequences (n = 21,401), exact duplicate evidence (n = 33,447), truncated extraction fragments (n = 30), and other quality-filtered records. Of the 250,269 effective (non-rejected) records, 26.5% have been manual_checked, 42.1% auto_imported, and 2.3% await review (needs_review). All evidence records carry provenance links to their source publications (92.0% PMID, 90.4% DOI), enabling users to independently verify any extracted claim against the primary literature. We emphasize that automated evidence grades are intended for triage and hypothesis generation, not as a substitute for expert evaluation of individual virus–host associations.

A complementary `host_association_method` field in the `infection_records` table (n = 9,548) provides finer-grained categorization: confirmed_infection (n = 842, 8.8%), pathology_observation (n = 4), disease_outbreak (n = 2,271, 23.8%), co_occurrence_metagenomic (n = 835, 8.7%), database_annotation (n = 781, 8.2%), phylum_inference (n = 360), ncbi_annotation (n = 317), name_inference (n = 44), and environmental_sample (n = 1).

**Curation Audit Trail.** All data modifications are recorded in the `curation_logs` table, capturing entity type (virus, isolate, protein, host, evidence), entity identifier, action type (insert, update, delete, promote), old and new values, curator identity (script name or human curator), and timestamp. The `data_provenance` table (100,599 entries) links each record to its source database (NCBI GenBank CDS features: 22,823; ICTV VMR: 19,271; ICTV Taxonomy: 17,554; UniProt REST API: 11,351; GBIF API: 4,039; KEGG REST API: 3,738; NCBI GenBank: 3,679; OBIS API: 2,885; STRING API: 993; bioRxiv API: 786; and others) with confidence level annotations. Data conflicts—instances where two sources provide contradictory values for the same entity and field—are recorded in the `curation_conflicts` table (1,245 entries) with severity grading (high/medium/low) and resolution status tracking.

### 7. Database Deployment and Web Interface

**REST API and Full-Text Search.** A REST API was implemented using FastAPI (Python 3.12) [64] providing programmatic access to all public database views. Endpoints include `/viruses` (query by name, family, genome type, host_phylum, discovery_context), `/hosts` (query by scientific_name, phylum, class, aquaculture_status), `/evidence` (filterable by strength tier, evidence_type, virus_master_id, host_id, reference_id), `/isolates` (by accession, master_id, taxon_family), `/proteins` (by functional_category, domain, accession), and `/references` (by pmid, doi, year, journal). Full-text search across virus names, host names, taxonomy fields, and geographic locations is implemented via SQLite FTS5 with the `virus_search_fts` virtual table indexing the `virus_master` table. API responses are returned in JSON format with pagination (default page_size=50, max=500) and CORS headers enabled for cross-origin access. Interactive API documentation is generated via Swagger UI (`/docs`) and ReDoc (`/redoc`).

**Docker Containerization and Deployment.** The complete application stack is containerized via Docker Compose (docker-compose.yml), comprising three services: (1) a PostgreSQL 16 database with health check, persistent volume storage, and automated initialization from the provided schema DDL and seed data; (2) a FastAPI application server (Python 3.12-slim base image) with Uvicorn ASGI server (4 workers), connecting to PostgreSQL via SQLAlchemy with connection pooling; and (3) an Nginx reverse proxy (alpine) providing rate limiting (10 requests/second per IP), static file serving for the web frontend, download directory indexing, and HTTPS termination (Let's Encrypt via certbot in production). Deployment requires a single command (`docker compose up -d`) after setting the database password environment variable.

**Data Download and Licensing.** Complete database snapshots are provided as five TSV files (117.6 MB total): virus_master.tsv (934 records), crustacean_hosts.tsv (183 records), viral_isolates.tsv (10,430 records), evidence_records.tsv (331,030 records), and ref_literatures.tsv (8,511 records). Files are UTF-8 encoded, tab-delimited, with header rows. An accompanying README documents the data dictionary for each table, evidence tier definitions, exclusion criteria for non-target records (n = 380 excluded from public export), and examples of API usage. All data are released under the CC-BY 4.0 license. The database will be deposited in Zenodo with a versioned DOI upon publication, ensuring long-term archival and citability. Source code for the data processing pipeline, database schema, and deployment configuration is available at [repository URL TBD].

**Discovery Context.** Nearly all viruses in AquaVir-KB were identified through sequencing-based approaches: 536 (32.5%) from metagenomic surveys of host-associated samples, 478 (29.0%) from environmental metagenomics, 336 (20.4%) from the ICTV Master Species List, 224 (13.6%) from metagenomics with explicit host evidence, and 48 (2.9%) from RdRp assembly-based discovery. We emphasize that 99.8% of catalogued viruses are known exclusively from sequence data and have not been isolated in culture. This distribution reflects the contemporary reality of aquatic invertebrate virology, where metagenomic discovery has vastly outpaced classical virological characterization, and users should calibrate their interpretation of virus–host associations accordingly. The Serratus project's petabase-scale alignment of 5.7 million SRA samples [12] and the Tara Oceans expedition's global-scale characterization of marine RNA virus diversity [13, 14] represent the current frontier of aquatic virus discovery.

### 3. Genome and Proteome Features

**Genome Size Distribution.** Genome lengths among the 7,842 isolates with sequence data range from 16 bp (fragments of circular ssDNA viruses) to 53.3 Mb (a misassembled metagenomic contig incorrectly attributed to a single viral genome), with a mean of 120.4 kb and a median of 9.2 kb. After filtering for complete genomes (n = 2,587 with genome accession numbers), the size distribution reflects viral genome type: ssRNA(+) genomes cluster around 7–12 kb, dsDNA genomes range from 100–400 kb (Malacoherpesviridae, Nimaviridae) to >1 Mb (giant Nucleocytoviricota), and ssDNA genomes typically fall in the 1–6 kb range. GC content varies widely both within and between families, from 28% in certain parvoviruses to 65% in some nucleocytoplasmic large DNA viruses, consistent with known family-level GC composition patterns.

**Protein Length and Functional Distribution.** The viral proteome of 26,894 proteins spans 19 to 7,195 amino acids (mean: 373 aa). Functional category assignment (Table 4) reveals a proteome dominated by replication-associated functions. RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRP) is the most abundant functional category (5,809 proteins, 21.6%), reflecting both the dominance of RNA viruses in the database and the essential role of RdRP as the catalytic core of RNA virus replication. Structural proteins (capsid, envelope glycoprotein, nucleocapsid, matrix, spike, and virion components) constitute 14.7% of the proteome (3,960 proteins). Replication-associated non-RdRP proteins (helicases, proteases, methyltransferases, nucleases, nucleotidyltransferases, and transcription factors) account for an additional 14.5% (3,904 proteins). Metabolism-associated proteins—predominantly nucleotide metabolism enzymes including dUTPases, thymidine kinases, ribonucleotide reductases, and deoxycytidylate deaminases—account for 4.1% (1,090 proteins). These large DNA virus-encoded metabolic enzymes are particularly enriched in Nucleocytoviricota, Malacoherpesviridae, and Nimaviridae, reflecting the metabolic reprogramming strategies of large dsDNA viruses. Host-interaction proteins (apoptosis regulators, ubiquitin ligases, immune evasion factors, RNA silencing suppressors) constitute 1.4% (365 proteins). Assembly-related proteins (terminases, holins, scaffolding proteins) are the smallest category (14 proteins, 0.1%).

Protein length varies systematically with functional category: RdRP sequences average 1,147 amino acids (range: 132–2,851), reflecting the conserved catalytic core with variable N-terminal domains. Structural proteins show the greatest length variability (mean: 278 aa, range: 19–7,195), with capsid proteins ranging from small (50–100 aa) for certain ssDNA viruses to very large (>3,000 aa) for giant virus major capsid proteins. Metabolism-associated proteins average 375 amino acids, consistent with the size of typical nucleotide metabolism enzymes.

### 4. Literature Evidence and Virus–Host Association Records

**Evidence Scale and Traceability.** The evidence layer represents AquaVir-KB's core differentiator. The database contains 353,160 structured evidence records (250,269 effective after excluding 102,891 rejected records) derived from 9,065 peer-reviewed publications covering a 76-year publication window (1950–2026). Literature traceability is a cornerstone: 92.1% of references carry PubMed IDs and 90.4% carry DOIs, enabling direct verification of evidence claims against primary sources. The temporal distribution reflects the growth of aquatic invertebrate virology: the 2010s contributed 2,881 references (33.9%), and the 2020s (through June 2026) have already contributed 3,840 references (45.1%).

**Evidence Type Distribution.** Evidence records are classified into ten types: host range (n = 141,749, 40.1%), diagnosis (n = 112,092, 31.7%), pathogenicity (n = 66,879, 18.9%), temperature (n = 25,631, 7.3%), other (n = 2,640), mortality (n = 1,775), natural infection (n = 1,264), transmission (n = 856), outbreak (n = 152), and virulence (n = 122).

**Evidence Strength Stratification.** The automated evidence grading reflects the distribution of information-source signals in text-mined records: 46,033 records (13.0%) are classified as high-grade (source publication reports experimental infection, virus isolation, or TEM pathology observation, as identified by keyword matching), 303,337 (85.9%) as medium-grade (source publication mentions a specific virus–host association with molecular or pathological context), and 3,790 (1.1%) as low-grade (metagenomic co-occurrence or computational inference without host confirmation). An additional 102,891 records (0.7%) are rejected—comprising truncated extraction fragments (n=30), strict evidence duplicates (n=2,324), and other quality-filtered records. Of the 250,269 effective records, curation status is distributed as: 42.1% auto_imported, 26.5% manual_checked, 2.3% needs_review. All evidence grades are assigned automatically; users should verify critical records against their linked source publications. The evidence quality distribution has been systematically improved through iterative quality hardening including multi-factor scoring, cross-reference triangulation, and duplicate suppression.

For the nine most intensively studied virus–host model systems, evidence quality is substantially elevated above the database-wide average (Table 5). WSSV, the most extensively studied aquatic invertebrate virus, has 17.0% high-grade evidence among 10,550 associated records, reflecting over three decades of experimental infection research, including early fulfillment of Koch's postulates for shrimp viruses [4] and detailed mechanistic studies of viral pathogenesis. YHV (7.0% high-grade among 2,008 records) and IHHNV (7.1% among 4,159 records) similarly benefit from decades of experimental characterization. The lower high-grade fractions for HaHV-1 (2.2%) and TSV (3.0%) reflect the more recent recognition of these pathogens and, in the case of HaHV-1, the challenges of working with abalone as experimental hosts. Comprehensive reviews of shrimp viral diseases in Asia [30] and the Americas [31], mollusk herpesvirus epidemiology [36, 37, 38], abalone ganglioneuritis pathobiology [39, 41], and emerging nodavirus infections in echinoderms [40] provide the primary literature foundation for these model systems. For parvoviruses of marine shrimp, detailed genomic characterization has been achieved for IHHNV [32], while the nodavirus pathogens MrNV and CMNV have been comprehensively reviewed [35]. The genetic diversification of IMNV across geographic regions has been documented [34], and the structural biology of YHV (Roniviridae) proteins has been partially characterized [33].

Tissue tropism data are available for isolates with documented isolation sources: hepatopancreas, gill, hemolymph, muscle, and lymphoid organ are the most frequently sampled tissues.

### 5. Virus Isolate and Protein Detail Records

The isolate detail layer provides per-accession granularity for each virus species. Among 11,884 isolates, the AccessionVersion format follows GenBank conventions (e.g., NC_003225.1, AF273215.1). Isolate-level metadata include genome length (available for 7,842 isolates), GC content, molecule type, sequence completeness status, and associated BioProject and BioSample identifiers (195 BioSample links established). Cross-reference links to external databases—including NCBI Nucleotide, NCBI Protein, UniProt, KEGG, and InterPro—are maintained in the `external_xrefs` table (3,492 entries).

The protein detail layer integrates, for each of the 26,894 proteins, multiple annotation dimensions: domain architecture from NCBI CDD (65,943 assignments across all proteins), InterPro domain classifications (427 proteins), GO term mappings (3,452 annotations assigning 176 unique GO identifiers to proteins), KEGG pathway links (4,294 protein-pathway associations across 924 pathways), and UniProt cross-references with standardized nomenclature (11,351 proteins). The most frequently assigned GO terms are cellular_component terms associated with virion structure: viral envelope (GO:0019031, 499 annotations), viral capsid (GO:0019028, 248 annotations), and virion component (GO:0044423, 152 annotations). Molecular_function terms are led by ATP binding (GO:0005524, 199 annotations), zinc ion binding (GO:0008270, 147 annotations), and RNA-directed RNA polymerase activity (GO:0003968, 91 annotations). Three-dimensional structural models (ESMFold) are available for 52 representative proteins.

### 6. Phylogenetic and Evolutionary Relationships

**RdRp-Based Classification.** Phylogenetic analysis of 131 representative RdRp sequences from unclassified or ambiguously classified RNA viruses yielded family-level classifications across 18 viral families. The IQ-TREE maximum-likelihood trees, with automatic model selection via ModelFinder and 1,000 ultrafast bootstrap replicates, provided robust support (bootstrap ≥70%, SH-aLRT ≥80%) for classification into Sedoreoviridae, Roniviridae, Dicistroviridae, Picornaviridae, Marnaviridae, Solemoviridae, Flaviviridae, Totiviridae, and nine additional families. For cases where phylogenetic signal was insufficient for confident family-level assignment (bootstrap <70%), the classification was recorded at the next-highest taxonomic rank (order or class) with a `low` confidence flag. Classification results are stored in the `rdrp_classification_v2` table with FastTree SH support values, IQ-TREE ultrafast bootstrap support, consensus ratios, and the number of known reference sequences in each clade.

**Taxonomic Orphan Rate.** The 68 viruses lacking family-level classification (7.3% of 934 species) represent an important frontier. These taxonomic orphans disproportionately derive from Porifera (46% unclassified), Cnidaria (28% unclassified), and Echinodermata (22% unclassified)—phyla for which reference virological data are sparse—suggesting that many of these sequences may represent previously undescribed viral lineages restricted to non-arthropod aquatic invertebrate hosts.

### 7. Database Access and User Interface

**Web Interface and API.** AquaVir-KB provides a REST API with interactive documentation via Swagger UI and ReDoc. The API supports parameterized queries across all public database views, with response pagination and JSON output. Full-text search enables rapid retrieval across virus names, host names, taxonomy, and geographic fields.

**Data Download.** Five TSV files (117.6 MB) provide the complete public dataset. A comprehensive README documents the data dictionary, evidence tier definitions, exclusion criteria for non-target records, and example API usage patterns.

**Geographic and Ecological Coverage.** Sample collection metadata span 48 countries across seven continents, with the strongest coverage in Asia (1,329 records; China: 202, Thailand: 89, India: 77, Philippines: 77, Vietnam: 38), North America (428; Canada: 216, Mexico: 147, United States: 36), and Oceania (64; Australia: 53). This distribution reflects the geographic concentration of aquaculture production and, consequently, virological surveillance effort. Temperature profiling data are available for 178 virus–host combinations, and virulence profiles for an additional 178 combinations, primarily for crustacean pathogens.

**Metagenomic Discovery Resource.** Metadata for 2,598 SRA runs from aquatic invertebrate metagenomic and metatranscriptomic studies are indexed in the database. NCBI Taxonomy Analysis identified 400 runs with detectable viral sequences: 1,282 mollusk-associated, 84 coral-associated, 116 crustacean (2024–2026), and 1 echinoderm-associated run. These represent a curated discovery queue for systematic viral genome mining.

## Discussion

**Paragraph 1: Significance of a Literature-Mined Evidence Index for Aquatic Invertebrate Virology.** AquaVir-KB fills a long-standing gap in virology informatics by providing the first systematically organized, literature-traceable evidence index dedicated to aquatic invertebrate viruses. The database's primary contribution is methodological rather than merely aggregative: by making explicit the evidentiary basis and provenance of each virus–host association through a transparent, multi-dimensional stratification (evidence origin: primary/secondary/database; evidence strength: high/medium/low; curation status: manual_checked/auto_imported/needs_review/rejected), AquaVir-KB transforms what is currently implicit in the literature—and entirely absent from sequence databases—into structured, queryable metadata. For a field in which 99.8% of known viruses have been discovered through sequencing rather than classical isolation, the distinction between a Koch's postulate-confirmed pathogen and a metagenomic co-occurrence has immediate practical consequences for biosecurity policy, aquaculture disease management, and research prioritization. A "low" evidence grade or "secondary" evidence origin does not imply an incorrect association; rather, it signals that the association represents a computationally or literature-mined relationship that awaits experimental validation—serving as a hypothesis-generation resource for researchers investigating emerging diseases in aquaculture species. We emphasize that 95.3% of evidence records derive from automated literature mining (secondary evidence) rather than primary experimental observation or manual expert curation, and users should calibrate their confidence accordingly.

**Paragraph 2: Positioning Among Existing Resources.** AquaVir-KB complements rather than duplicates existing virus databases (Supplementary Table S1). It serves a fundamentally different purpose from sequence repositories (NCBI Virus, GenBank), taxonomic authorities (ICTV VMR), and computational inference platforms (Virus-Host DB). The database's unique value proposition lies in the combination of (i) explicit, transparent evidence stratification (origin, strength, and curation status) applied uniformly across all records, (ii) per-record literature provenance (92.0% PMID, 90.4% DOI) enabling direct verification against primary sources, (iii) phylum-specific scope for aquatic invertebrates with explicit host ecological classification, and (iv) integration of multi-dimensional data—taxonomy, genomics, proteomics, phylogenetics, geography, and ecology—within a single relational framework. In comparing AquaVir-KB to existing resources, we note that NCBI Virus provides the broadest sequence coverage and GenBank records carry host qualifiers (/host) for many accessions; the ICTV VMR provides authoritative taxonomic classification; and Virus-Host DB computationally infers host associations at scale. AquaVir-KB's contribution is complementary: it adds literature traceability, evidence quality stratification, and phylum-specific curation that these resources do not provide, while acknowledging that its coverage (1,647 species) is smaller than comprehensive sequence repositories. Quantitatively, the database captures 40 ICTV-listed viruses absent from the initial build through systematic gap analysis, including 22 newly classified starfish parvoviruses that no other integrated resource has yet incorporated.

**Paragraph 3: Limitations and Their Implications.** Several limitations of the current release warrant transparent acknowledgment to guide appropriate use and to address concerns raised during peer review.

*Evidence provenance.* Of 353,160 evidence records, 95.3% (n = 336,512) are classified as secondary evidence derived from automated literature mining (`smart_match_re_extract` pipeline), 3.7% (n = 13,121) are primary evidence from laboratory and field studies, and 1.0% (n = 3,527) are database-derived annotations. The secondary evidence records are extracted through keyword and regular expression matching against publication titles and abstracts; they have not undergone systematic manual verification. Users should treat secondary evidence as literature-derived assertions requiring independent confirmation rather than as verified facts. The evidence grading (high/medium/low) is assigned automatically by a keyword-based decision tree and should be interpreted as a preliminary signal, not a definitive expert assessment. Additionally, because the same experimental finding may be cited by multiple publications, evidence records may represent duplicate counts of the same underlying observation across citing papers.

*Data accessibility.* Public visibility is tiered: 1,254 of 1,647 active viruses (76.1%) are publicly accessible (Tier 1), 347 (21.1%) are limited-access entries (Tier 2: predominantly partial_genome and literature_candidate entries without curated profiles), and 46 (2.8%) are internal-only entries (Tier 3: host_phylum=unknown or unconfirmed_candidate records). While Tier 2 entries are available upon registration, NAR Database Issue policy requires fully open access; we are working toward promoting additional entries to Tier 1 following curated profile completion. The current production web deployment, Docker image, and Zenodo DOI are in preparation and will be finalized before resubmission.

*Host phylum scope.* Following a systematic taxonomic audit prompted by peer review, we reclassified 54 Nematoda-associated viruses as non-target after determining that all represented terrestrial agricultural pest nematodes (potato rot nematode, soybean cyst nematode, root-knot nematode, etc.) rather than aquatic free-living species. Two Rotifera entries were also reclassified (Aedes birnavirus → non_aquatic; Redspotted grouper nervous necrosis virus → non_target vertebrate), reducing Rotifera from 4 to 2 confirmed entries. The "multiple phyla" category (n = 336) consists exclusively of ICTV Master Species List entries with documented cross-phylum host ranges; 30 environmental metagenomic detections previously classified as "multiple" were reclassified to "unknown." The "unknown" category (n = 73) warrants careful interpretation: these viruses were detected in environmental samples and have no confirmed host association.

*Evidence coverage disparity.* Evidence per virus is highly skewed: 795 viruses (48.3% of those with evidence) have only a single evidence record, and 1,018 have five or fewer. Only 167 viruses (10.1% of those with evidence) have any primary (laboratory or field) evidence. The four isolated-and-cultured viruses (WSSV, YHV, TSV, IHHNV) account for a disproportionate share of laboratory evidence, with 22.5–40.4% of their evidence records derived from laboratory studies compared to 3.7% database-wide. Users should not interpret a high evidence count for a well-studied virus as implying equivalent evidentiary depth for less-studied viruses.

*Phylogenetic and protein annotation limitations.* Sixty-eight viruses (4.1%) lack family-level classification and remain as taxonomic orphans. Protein functional annotation is predominantly domain-inferred (87.9%) via NCBI CDD batch search, with 12.1% of proteins remaining unannotated. These annotations are computational predictions, not experimental validations.

*Data quality.* While all 837 originally identified P0 (blocking-level) QAQC issues have been resolved, 5,303 P1 (non-blocking) issues remain, predominantly `evidence_claim_text_pollution` (n = 5,000) requiring re-extraction of concise claims from noisy extraction fragments. An additional 12,682 evidence records await manual review. Of 9,065 references, 524 (5.8%) lack both PMID and DOI, predominantly grey literature (institutional reports, GenBank Direct Submissions) that cannot be verified through standard bibliographic databases. A total of 1,349 evidence records have NULL reference_id and 22,469 records reside in the deduplication quarantine pending entity resolution.

**Paragraph 4: Future Development Roadmap.** AquaVir-KB is designed as an evolving resource, and several development trajectories are planned for the next release cycle. (i) SRA-based virus discovery: systematic RdRP HMM profiling against assembled contigs from the 2,598 indexed SRA runs, modeled on the Serratus approach that identified >100,000 novel RNA viruses from public SRA data [12], with conservative estimates suggesting 500–1,500 additional virus species may be recoverable from under-sampled mollusk, coral, and echinoderm metatranscriptomes. (ii) Chinese literature integration: development of a Chinese-language NLP pipeline targeting CNKI-indexed journals, with an initial focus on mollusk and sea cucumber disease studies. (iii) Phylogenetic orphan resolution: systematic RdRP and VP1 phylogenetic placement of the 68 unclassified viruses using the established MAFFT + IQ-TREE pipeline calibrated against expanded ICTV reference taxa. (iv) Real-time updating: transition from a static release model to automated weekly ingestion of new viral accessions (NCBI GenBank), new publications (Europe PMC), and new metagenomic runs (NCBI SRA). (v) Community curation interface: a web-based platform enabling domain experts to review, correct, and augment evidence records with a reputation-weighted voting system, following models successfully implemented by community-curated biological databases (e.g., UniProt, RNAcentral). (vi) Phylum expansion: targeted data collection for currently unrepresented aquatic invertebrate phyla (Nemertea, Bryozoa, Chaetognatha, Tardigrada, Hemichordata) and for the meiofaunal fraction of marine invertebrate biodiversity, which remains almost entirely unexplored virologically.

## Supplementary Information

The 2025 NAR Database Issue collection [54] documents the continued growth of molecular biology databases, within which AquaVir-KB occupies a previously unfilled niche. The database's functional annotation pipeline leverages Pfam [49] for core gene identification and ultrafast bootstrap approximation [51] for phylogenetic support assessment. Geographic host occurrence data were integrated from GBIF [56] and OBIS [57], while SRA-derived viral genome quality was assessed using CheckV [53]. Future releases will incorporate automated virus discovery tools including VirSorter2 [61], Cenote-Taker 2 [60], and VIBRANT [52] for enhanced metagenomic mining. The expansion of known ssRNA phage genomes from tens to thousands [59] and the unprecedented genomic diversity revealed by arthropod RNA virus surveys [65] underscore the discovery potential remaining in under-sampled aquatic invertebrate taxa, and will align with emerging frameworks for aquatic food safety risk assessment [62, 63].
